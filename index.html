Thoughts on Progressive Enhancement

After reading two articles on Progressive Enhancement, I have really understood how it works and personally agree with it. I think every website is important to have rich HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. When we surf the web there are hundreds of thousands of websites that are visited every day, and some just lack the idea of Progressive Enhancement. You can tell which website has all these components and which doesn't. In the article by Aaron Gustafson, his Peanut M&M analogy really makes sense to describe this idea. It really gives a good description of what a website should have. He describes the HTML(Content) as the peanut, a very large index of HTML that builds the website to its potential. Next, he describes the chocolate coating as a thin but tasty layer of CSS. When we add CSS to a website, it needs to be complex and minimal in my opinion. CSS should not overdo the website, but it should be very soft and understanding. Lastly, he describes JavaScript as a hard candy shell. JavaScript makes up the most important part of the website providing the user with a unique experience. If our JavaScript was lacking or missing then the M&M wouldn't have an outer shell, which would make it just a peanut covered in chocolate. Aaron Gustafson did a great job at providing an explanation and understanding of Progressive Enhancement, but I found another article as well.

This link for this next article is listed below.

Progressive Enhancement: What It Is, And How To Use It?

This article gives a good example of a little Navigation box and how adding Progressive Enhancement can make it look more understanding and user-friendly. This article guides the reader through each step showing how Progressive Enhancement works, and why it is important. The last paragraph from this article really spoke to how I understand Progressive Enhancement and I will just quote it here "clearly establishing how a system’s interactive elements are supposed to function at the most basic level, you gain a clearer understanding of the project’s requirements. Your clients will be happy with the rock-solid results, the more accurate time-frame estimates, and the easier maintenance, and you will be happy knowing you’ve provided a better, stronger product that benefits more people." Sam Dwyer, SmashingMagazine.com, Apr 22, 2009

I really enjoyed reading these two articles and really understand and agree with Progressive Enhancement, Sam Dwyer and Aaron Gustafson really sum this concept up in different but very convincing ways.
